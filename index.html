Citibank India · 0→1 Pilot → Live Launch

Suvidha Digital: taking salary account opening from paper to platform

Led the end-to-end pilot of a digital account-opening product that eliminated manual fraud risks, cut entity onboarding from 10+ days to under 48 hours, and went live across select cities.

10d → 48hEntity onboarding time for new corporates
ZeroPhysical document handling. The primary fraud vector, removed
LiveShipped from pilot to production across select cities
The problem: a pre-digital process meeting a 2020 world

Suvidha is Citibank's salary account product for employees of corporate partners like TCS, L&T Mindtree and EY. The entire process was manual: kits checked out of the bank vault, Aadhaar and PAN documents physically handled at corporate sites, paper forms with transcription errors, and an outsourced FATCA check on every application. COVID made on-site collection impossible overnight, and I was given the mandate to build and pilot the digital replacement.

The hardest failure point: large Indian corporates operate as multiple legal entities (EY India, EY LLP, EY GDS…), each needing its own organisation code. If a banker visited an unregistered entity, listing it took 10+ days, and the customer was lost.

The product decisions that made it work
  • Real-time entity tagging: organisation code lookup reflecting the latest onboarded entities with no lag.
  • Dummy code + HR attestation fallback: unrecognised entities got a 0000 code with an attested approval doc, preserving the application instead of losing the customer at the point of highest intent.
  • 48-hour entity onboarding SLA: the hardest internal negotiation; won by reframing it as revenue protection, not process change.
  • Inline FATCA: declaration built into the digital form, eliminating the third-party outsourcing step entirely.

Every pilot iteration was driven by a specific field failure reported by bankers. Real feedback over internal opinion, every time.

American Express · via EY · Compliance Platform

DLM Gold Simulation: preventing compliance failures before they happen

Shipped a dry-run capability for Amex's Data Lifecycle Management platform that cut execution failures by ~50% and gave compliance teams ~90% scheduling accuracy for the first time.

~50%Reduction in annual DLM production failures
~90%Execution-time estimation accuracy, enabling optimized scheduling
7 to 9 daysEnd-to-end compliance cycle the failures used to burn
The problem: failures only surfaced when it was already too late

DLM is Amex's compliance-critical deletion utility, the system that answers "when is data no longer allowed to exist, and how do we prove we deleted it?" The full cycle took 7 to 9 days of cross-functional effort, and the only failure signal was a post-execution email. Unoptimized SQL, memory exhaustion and misconfigured rules surfaced with zero prior warning; teams were building informal buffer time into compliance calendars just to absorb re-runs.

The product thinking behind the fix
  • Shell script over a UI: plugged into existing user workflows with zero new infrastructure or access provisioning. Adoption speed beat elegance.
  • Execution-time output as a core feature: not just pass/fail; it solved the user's problem and the DLM team's scheduling problem simultaneously.
  • Self-serve testing: users could validate SQL independently, removing the DLM team as a single point of dependency.
  • Supported rollout: a squad-assisted launch model meant the utility went live with no support-ticket spike.

Prioritisation rule: every user complaint was mapped to prevent-failure, reduce-dependency, or improve-scheduling. Features hitting all three shipped first.

American Express · AI Platform Enablement

Enabling GenAI at scale: 72 data pipelines into LUMI

Coordinated the migration of 72 data files from business units across the bank into LUMI, Amex's next-gen cloud-native AI platform, treating migration as a product problem, not a technical task.

72Data pipelines validated for quality, governance and ingestion standards
$300MAnnual AWS spend the platform was built to solve
15,000+Internal data users relying on the platform downstream
Why this was a product problem

Most AI failures happen at the data layer, not the model layer. A state-of-the-art GenAI model fed stale or wrongly-permissioned data produces outputs that are dangerous because they carry false confidence. Migration had users (the AI systems), requirements (governance standards), stakeholders (72 business-unit owners) and outcomes (GenAI use cases like real-time personalised offers that worked or failed on what we delivered).

  • Sequenced migration by downstream AI use-case priority, not department order.
  • Handled quality gaps with time-bound remediation paths instead of blocking the platform.
  • Completed governance metadata (lineage, privacy classification, access permissions) for every dataset before ingestion.
